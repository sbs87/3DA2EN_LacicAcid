Hey Philippe,

I just had an awesome idea. If you’re bored over the weekend, and want to try your programming skills out, why don’t you come up with a little script that outputs a “Quality Control (QC)” call for each of our samples? I’ve attached a bioanalyzer summary table in numbers and also an easy-to-parse tab delimited format (metrics taken from the traces)... which can be used as the input to the script and then you can make the script output a single quality call for each sample. 

So, after reading in each sample’s metrics, it should output a quality call., with the call being either “Good”, “Ok” and “Bad”.  

I’d say a GOOD sample has:
-Concentration greater than 10 ng/ul (just for the hell of it)
-A RIN > 8
-A 28s/18s > 1

An “ok” sample has:
-Concentration greater than 10 ng/ul
-A RIN < 8 OR a 28s/18s < 1 (but not both)

A “bad” sample has:
-Concentration less than 10 ng/ul OR (RIN <8 AND 28s/18s < 1).  Notice the difference between a bad and ok sample. A bad sample has crappy RIN AND ratio while an ok has just a crappy RIN OR ratio, but not both. The concentration should be greater than 10 to be considered at least ok. 

Feel free to play around with what makes a sample good, ok and bad (i.e., the thresholds of 10, 8, 1) … these are just numbers that kind of feel right. And if you’re really feeling up for it, make an output table that shows the replicate calls side by side, and then gives an overall call for each sample together (for instance, an ok and a good sample is ok overall while a bad and ok sample is still ok). 

This is only if you’re feeling up to it and want a little project over the weekend. It’d be helpful to get a feel for what kinds of applications you can make for what you’ve been learning. If not/too busy/lazy, I’ll make the QC calls so its no big deal (probably by hand, but this screams script especially if you’re going to do it repeatedly for a large # of samples). 

Also, feel free to double check that I transcribed the trace info correctly. I didnt check yet.
